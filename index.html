A real paper from 1982, by the physicist John Hopfield, describing the kind of memory that brings back a whole song from two notes. The paper is now stored inside the memory it describes: every speck of ink below is one switch in the network. The idea won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics.

What you just did is where AI's memory comes from.

A chatbot pulling up the one thing you told it that matters, right when it matters, is making the move you just made with your finger on a 45-year-old page. It has a name: attention. Stacking it, over and over, is what lets ChatGPT and Claude hold a conversation.

Their version runs in shades of grey rather than plain on/off — the continuous form of the same recall step.

The one equation — and why it is physics

E  =  −1⁄2  ∑∑i≠j  Tij Vi Vj Equation [7], page 2556

What it says. Each V is one switch — on or off. (Hopfield shows this is isomorphic to a spin: a tiny magnet pointing up or down.) Each Tij is the strength of the link between two switches — how much they “want” to agree. E sums this over every pair: a single number for how much the whole network disagrees with its own wiring.

How it works. The recall rule only ever flips a switch in a way that lowers E, never raises it — Hopfield proves this (equation [8]). So the network slides downhill and stops at a nearby low point — “a least (local) E,” in his words. Those low points are the stored memories. A scribbled page is a ball on a hillside; you let go, and it rolls to the nearest valley floor.

The valleys have a name — and Hopfield names them. On page 2557, in italics: “The phase space flow is apparently dominated by attractors which are the nominally assigned memories, each of which dominates a substantial region around it.” An attractor: many different starts, one shared ending, because the dynamics pull everything in a basin to the same floor. Scribble the same page twice, two completely different ways, and watch both scribbles end at the identical print. The field this paper started came to be called attractor networks — and the sentence that named the attractors is stored on a page above, inside one.

Why it is physics — in his own words. This energy is not a metaphor for physics; it is physics. On the page above, Hopfield writes:

“This case is isomorphic with an Ising model. Tij provides the role of the exchange coupling… When Tij is symmetric but has a random character (the spin glass) there are known to be many (locally) stable states.”

The Ising model is physicists' model of a magnet; a spin glass is a disordered magnet with many stable configurations. The step this paper takes is to read those stable configurations as memories, which puts recall and a magnetic system relaxing to a nearby low-energy state under the same mathematics — the same E, the same descent, the same local minima. That is the whole theory; everything on this page is that one idea running.

The one equation here that is not from 1982

What has to be decided. Everything above says what Tij does. Nothing says how to choose it. That is the one open slot in the theory, and it is the only slot this page fills differently from the paper.

Tij  =  ∑s  ξis ξjs 1982 — equation [2], page 2555

Hopfield's rule, in one line: for every stored page s, add +1 to the link between two pixels that agree in that page and −1 where they disagree. (ξ is just a pixel written as ±1 instead of on/off.) Wire agreement into the weights, and the pattern becomes a valley.

Tij  =  ∑s,r  ξis (G−1)sr ξjr 1985 — the projection rule (Personnaz–Guyon–Dreyfus), with Gsr = ξs·ξr

Look at what changed. One factor. G is the table of overlaps: Gsr counts how much page s and page r already look alike. Set G to the identity — every memory unrelated to every other — and the second equation collapses back into the first. Hopfield's rule is the 1985 rule under the assumption that the memories don't overlap.

Why that assumption breaks here. On random memories it is nearly true, which is why the 1982 rule is the right tool for the capacity experiment beside p. 2557. But these five pages are the same journal, the same typeface, the same wide white margins — they overlap enormously. Each page's ink then leaks into every other page's weights, and p. 2557 states what follows: “memories too close to each other are confused and tend to merge.” Multiplying by G−1 divides that shared part back out.

What it buys. Written this way T is the projection onto the stored pages, so T ξs = ξs exactly — every stored page is a perfect fixed point however much the pages resemble each other. That is the entire difference. The rule rebuilding the print is still Eq [1], the energy pulling it downhill is still Eq [7], and the valleys are still attractors; only the choice of T is from 1985. The toggle in the panel swaps it back to Eq [2], so you can watch the merge happen.

Neither matrix is ever built. T would have 784,320² entries. The field on a pixel is computed from the five overlaps between the image on screen and the stored pages — the same arithmetic, one update at a time.

h is that sum. The threshold is gone because the paper sets it to zero itself — “Unless otherwise stated, we choose Ui = 0” — so “above threshold” is just h > 0. The third branch is the case the printed rule leaves out: at exactly the threshold, nothing changes.

That is the entire recall step. Everything else in the file is bookkeeping: which pixel to visit next, and keeping the five overlaps up to date so h is cheap to compute. 177 lines, and you can read all of them — hopfield.js. Nothing is stored anywhere except the equations and the print.

The four dates, with sources

  • 1985 — Personnaz, Guyon & Dreyfus, the projection rule: the storage recipe this page actually runs, and the reason five near-identical pages stay apart.

  • 2024 — Nobel Prize in Physics, Hopfield and Hinton.
The fine print — is this really the paper?
  • The storage rule is 1985, not 1982 — the projection rule, because five pages this similar merge under Eq [2]. Both equations are written out and compared above, in the one equation here that is not from 1982; the toggle on the right switches between them.
  • The network's-eye view is a pure black/white bitmap at reduced resolution, so tiny text looks rough — that is the memory, not a photo.
  • Scribble out more than half a page and it rolls into the photographic negative — an equally deep valley. Damage it past its basin and it can land on a different page. The readout names which one happened.
  • Pixels-as-switches is the standard way to picture a Hopfield network — the memory is a bitmap, not "understanding." It does not read the words as language.
  • Related earlier work, for anyone tracing the theory: Shun'ichi Amari (1972) and William Little (1974), whom Hopfield cites as refs. 6–8 and 25 on the last page above.
