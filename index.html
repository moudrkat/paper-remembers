What you just watched is the ancestor of how AI remembers.

The pull you saw — a damaged input snapping back to the nearest stored memory — is the classical 1982 form of this network. In 2020 the same network, generalized to continuous values, was shown to compute exactly “attention” — the core step inside transformer AI models like GPT and Claude. So today's chatbots recall context using a direct descendant of the mechanism you just played with: an idea that began as physics, about magnets and neurons.

How a memory lookup becomes “attention”

You handed the network a damaged page and it gave back the nearest complete page it had stored — a lookup by similarity. That is all attention is. As an AI reads, every earlier word is a stored memory; for the word it is on now, it pulls back the stored words most similar to that one, and blends them. Same move you just made — a cue fetching its closest memory — only with words instead of pages, and shades of grey instead of black and white. Stack that lookup millions of times and you have a language model.

The whole story, in four steps

  1. 1982 — Hopfield shows a network of simple on/off units can store memories and rebuild each one from just a fragment.
  2. 2024 — the idea wins the Nobel Prize in Physics (shared with Geoffrey Hinton).
  3. 1985 — Personnaz, Guyon & Dreyfus's projection rule lets one network store many overlapping memories and recall each without error. That is the rule this page runs.
  4. 2020 — the same network, generalized to continuous values (“Hopfield Networks Is All You Need”), is shown to compute exactly the attention step inside transformer AI models like GPT and Claude.

How it actually works. The page is a grid of on/off switches — one per pixel of ink. The clean page sits at the bottom of a "valley." When you scribble, you shove the switches up a hillside; let go, and they roll back down to the nearest valley bottom. Because that bottom is a stored page, the page returns. Nothing points it at the answer — it simply falls there. Five pages are stored in the same network, and it still finds the right one.

The honest details — is this really the paper?
  • Storing five whole pages in one network is the hard case. Hopfield's original 1982 rule can't do it — pages share so much white space they blur into one ghost (you can switch to that rule, right, and watch it happen). To make recall work this page uses the projection (pseudo-inverse) rule, a well-known 1985 improvement (Personnaz–Guyon–Dreyfus). Same network, same "roll downhill" dynamics, same energy — only the way memories are written into the weights is smarter.
  • The page runs at a reduced resolution (the network's-eye view is a pure black/white bitmap), so tiny text looks rough — that is the memory, not a photo.
  • Scribble out more than half a page and it rolls into the photographic negative — an equally deep valley. Damage it past its basin and it can land on a different page. Both are honest; the readout tells you.
  • Pixels-as-switches is the classic way to picture a Hopfield network — the memory is a bitmap, not "understanding." It does not read the words as language.
Re-run Hopfield's own Fig. 2 capacity experiment

Hopfield measured how recall breaks down as you cram in more memories. 100 switches, n random memories, his original 1982 rule. Push n past ~15 and recall falls apart — the very limit the 1985 projection rule above was built to push past.
