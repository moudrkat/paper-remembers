This is a real scientific paper from 1982, by the physicist John Hopfield. It describes a strange kind of memory — one that can rebuild the whole thing from just a piece of it, the way two notes can bring back a whole song. So we stored the paper inside that very memory. Every speck of ink is one tiny switch; rub any of it out, and it finds its way back. The idea won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics.

That move has a name: attention. Stacking it, over and over, is what lets ChatGPT and Claude hold a conversation. Their version works in shades of grey instead of plain on/off — but it's the same idea you're playing with.

The one equation — and why it is physics

E  =  −1⁄2  ∑∑i≠j  Tij Vi Vj Equation [7], page 2556

What it says. Each V is one switch — on or off. (Hopfield shows this is isomorphic to a spin: a tiny magnet pointing up or down.) Each Tij is the strength of the link between two switches — how much they “want” to agree. E sums this over every pair: a single number for how much the whole network disagrees with its own wiring.

How it works. The recall rule only ever flips a switch in a way that lowers E, never raises it — Hopfield proves this (equation [8]). So the network slides downhill and stops at a nearby low point — “a least (local) E,” in his words. Those low points are the stored memories. A scribbled page is a ball on a hillside; you let go, and it rolls to the nearest valley floor.

The valleys have a name — and Hopfield names them. On page 2557, in italics: “The phase space flow is apparently dominated by attractors which are the nominally assigned memories, each of which dominates a substantial region around it.” An attractor: many different starts, one shared ending, because the dynamics pull everything in a basin to the same floor. Scribble the same page twice, two completely different ways, and watch both scribbles end at the identical print. The field this paper started came to be called attractor networks — and the sentence that named the attractors is stored on a page above, inside one.

Why it is physics — in his own words. This energy is not a metaphor for physics; it is physics. On the page above, Hopfield writes:

“This case is isomorphic with an Ising model. Tij provides the role of the exchange coupling… When Tij is symmetric but has a random character (the spin glass) there are known to be many (locally) stable states.”

The Ising model is physicists' model of a magnet; a spin glass is a disordered magnet with many stable configurations. The step this paper takes is to read those stable configurations as memories, which puts recall and a magnetic system relaxing to a nearby low-energy state under the same mathematics — the same E, the same descent, the same local minima. That is the whole theory; everything on this page is that one idea running.

The four dates, with sources

  • 1982 — J. J. Hopfield, Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities, PNAS 79:2554–2558. The five pages above.
  • 1985 — Personnaz, Guyon & Dreyfus, the projection rule: the storage recipe this page actually runs, and the reason five near-identical pages stay apart.
  • 2020 — Ramsauer et al., Hopfield Networks is All You Need: the continuous version of the update above is transformer attention.
  • 2024 — Nobel Prize in Physics, Hopfield and Hinton.
The fine print — is this really the paper?
  • The storage rule is 1985, not 1982. The five pages share most of their white background, and p. 2557 states that "memories too close to each other are confused and tend to merge." Under Hopfield's 1982 rule (Eq [2]) they do merge — the toggle on the right runs that rule, and the pages come back as one ghost. This page therefore stores them with the projection (pseudo-inverse) rule (Personnaz–Guyon–Dreyfus, 1985), which makes every stored pattern an exact fixed point regardless of overlap. Same network, same Eq [1] dynamics, same Eq [7] energy; only how the memories enter the weights differs.
  • The network's-eye view is a pure black/white bitmap at reduced resolution, so tiny text looks rough — that is the memory, not a photo.
  • Scribble out more than half a page and it rolls into the photographic negative — an equally deep valley. Damage it past its basin and it can land on a different page. The readout names which one happened.
  • Pixels-as-switches is the standard way to picture a Hopfield network — the memory is a bitmap, not "understanding." It does not read the words as language.
  • Related earlier work, for anyone tracing the theory: Shun'ichi Amari (1972) and William Little (1974), whom Hopfield cites as refs. 6–8 and 25 on the last page above.
Re-run Hopfield's own Fig. 2 capacity experiment

Hopfield measured how recall breaks down as you cram in more memories. 100 switches, n random memories, his original 1982 rule. Push n past ~15 and recall falls apart — the very limit the 1985 projection rule above was built to push past.
