The paper that remembers itself

J. J. Hopfield, 1982 — Neural networks and physical systems with emergent collective computational abilities. It defines a memory whose stored patterns are minima of an energy: equation [7], page 2556. Here, four of the paper's printed equations are stored as memories in exactly that network — every pixel of print is a neuron. Damage the print, and the equation pulls itself back into place, downhill in its own energy.

The healing you just watched is the paper, not an animation of it: storage is the Hebbian prescription [2], the dynamics is rule [1] — asynchronous, random order — with the threshold choice from page 2557 (the μ = ±1 variables), and the number in the margin instrument is E of equation [7], which rule [1] can only ever decrease (equation [8]). The failure modes are honest physics too: corrupt past 50% and the print heals into its own photographic negative — the complement is an equally deep minimum. At exactly 50% it's a coin toss. Erase everything and it resurrects — blank paper still agrees with the print about the background, and that agreement is enough to fall back in. And each equation lives in its own network for the reason the paper gives on page 2557: the four strips share their white background, and "memories too close to each other are confused and tend to merge" — we tried; they do. A 2020 footnote: Ramsauer et al., Hopfield Networks is All You Need, proved the modern continuous version of this update rule is exactly transformer attention — the memory you just smudged is a direct ancestor of the mechanism inside every large language model.
