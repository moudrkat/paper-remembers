The one equation — and why it is physics

E  =  −1⁄2  ∑∑i≠j  Tij Vi Vj Equation [7], page 2556

What it says. Each V is one switch — on or off. (Hopfield shows this is isomorphic to a spin: a tiny magnet pointing up or down.) Each Tij is the strength of the link between two switches — how much they “want” to agree. E sums this over every pair: a single number for how much the whole network disagrees with its own wiring.

How it works. The recall rule only ever flips a switch in a way that lowers E, never raises it — Hopfield proves this (equation [8]). So the network slides downhill and stops at a nearby low point — “a least (local) E,” in his words. Those low points are the stored memories. A scribbled page is a ball on a hillside; you let go, and it rolls to the nearest valley floor.

Why it is physics — in his own words. This energy is not a metaphor for physics; it is physics. On the page above, Hopfield writes:

“This case is isomorphic with an Ising model. Tij provides the role of the exchange coupling… When Tij is symmetric but has a random character (the spin glass) there are known to be many (locally) stable states.”

The Ising model is physicists' model of a magnet; a spin glass is a disordered magnet with many stable configurations. Hopfield's leap was to see that those many stable configurations could be memories — so remembering becomes nothing more exotic than a magnetic system relaxing to a nearby low-energy state. That is why a paper about neurons won a Nobel Prize in Physics. The 2024 committee put his contribution plainly: he “created an associative memory that can store and reconstruct images and other types of patterns in data” — which is exactly what you just did to a page.

The whole story, plainly

  • 1982 — this paper. John Hopfield showed that a bunch of simple on/off switches, wired together, can store memories and rebuild any one of them from just a scrap. Everything you are playing with — the switches, the healing, the rolling-downhill-to-a-memory — is his.
  • 2024 — the Nobel. This idea won the Nobel Prize in Physics, shared with Geoffrey Hinton.
  • 1985 — the one upgrade you are running. Three researchers found a smarter way to set the wiring so many similar pages don't smear together. Nothing else changes — same healing, same idea. Flip the toggle on the right to switch back to Hopfield's original 1982 wiring and watch the pages smear.
  • 2020 — why it matters for AI. The same idea, tuned to work with shades of grey instead of on/off, turned out to be exactly the “attention” step inside AI chatbots like GPT and Claude. (That version is not what runs here — this is the original.)

The honest details — is this really the paper?
  • Storing five whole pages in one network is the hard case. Hopfield's original 1982 rule can't do it — pages share so much white space they blur into one ghost (you can switch to that rule, right, and watch it happen). To make recall work this page uses the projection (pseudo-inverse) rule, a well-known 1985 improvement (Personnaz–Guyon–Dreyfus). Same network, same "roll downhill" dynamics, same energy — only the way memories are written into the weights is smarter.
  • The page runs at a reduced resolution (the network's-eye view is a pure black/white bitmap), so tiny text looks rough — that is the memory, not a photo.
  • Scribble out more than half a page and it rolls into the photographic negative — an equally deep valley. Damage it past its basin and it can land on a different page. Both are honest; the readout tells you.
  • Pixels-as-switches is the classic way to picture a Hopfield network — the memory is a bitmap, not "understanding." It does not read the words as language.
  • Is the credit clean? Not entirely — and a tribute should say so. Similar ideas came earlier (Shun'ichi Amari, 1972; William Little, 1974, whom Hopfield cites), and some argue the model shouldn't carry his name alone. What Hopfield added — the energy-function framing and a telling clear enough to cross from physics into biology — is what made it take off. The 2024 physics Nobel for neural networks was itself debated as to whether it counts as physics. None of that makes the idea less beautiful.
